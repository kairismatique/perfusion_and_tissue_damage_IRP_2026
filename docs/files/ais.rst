Acute ischaemic stroke
======================

Stroke Physiology
-----------------

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), strokes are a leading cause of death worldwide.
In 2021, strokes were the **third** most common cause of death, and in 2000 they were the **second** most common.
This consistency highlights their significant impact on human health and the urgent need for effective treatments.

A stroke can be **ischaemic** or **haemorrhagic**.
Ischaemic strokes are the most common type, representing around **87%** of all cases.
They occur when a blood vessel is occluded by a clot, leading to partial or complete interruption of blood flow.

- A **thrombus** is a blood clot that forms at the site of the stroke.
- An **embolus** is a clot that forms elsewhere in a large artery and then travels until it lodges in a smaller vessel.

Stroke is not only a matter of mortality but also of disability.
In 2019, more than **100 million people** were living with stroke-related disabilities worldwide.
The consequences extend beyond the patient, affecting families and communities.
The economic burden is substantial, with the global cost of stroke treatment and associated disabilities estimated at **\$861 billion in 2019**.

What is an Acute Ischaemic Stroke?
----------------------------------

This thesis focuses specifically on **Acute Ischaemic Stroke (AIS)**.
A defining feature of AIS is the sudden onset of a clot, which rapidly reduces **perfusion** (the volume of blood delivered to tissue per unit time, measured in *ml/min/100g*).
The resulting shortage of blood supply leads to **oxygen deprivation** and **cell death**, known as an *infarct*.

Early treatment is essential to restore blood flow, limit brain damage, and reduce long-term disability.
Two main treatment approaches exist:

- **Thrombolysis**:
  Patients presenting within 4.5 hours of stroke onset may receive intravenous **tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)**, commonly Alteplase, to dissolve the clot.

- **Thrombectomy**:
  Selected patients presenting within 24 hours may undergo a mechanical procedure to remove a large clot.
  This involves inserting a catheter into the affected vessel to break up the clot and restore blood flow.

The **location of the clot** plays a critical role in the severity and outcome of stroke:

- **Left Middle Cerebral Artery Occlusion (LMCAo)**: often affects speech and language functions.
- **Right Middle Cerebral Artery Occlusion (RMCAo)**: commonly affects spatial awareness and attention, particularly on the left side of the body.

Both are medical emergencies requiring urgent intervention.

Signs of Acute Ischaemic Stroke
-------------------------------

The main signs of an AIS can be summarised as:

- **Facial paralysis**: one side of the face droops or feels numb.
- **Arm weakness**: weakness or numbness in one arm.
- **Speech difficulty**: difficulty speaking or understanding language.

If one or more of these symptoms occur, whether temporary or persistent, emergency services should be contacted immediately.
Prompt medical attention is crucial for recovery.
